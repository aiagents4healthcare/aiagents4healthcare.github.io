AI Agents for Healthcare Workshop

This workshop explores the cutting-edge intersection of AI agents and healthcare, focusing on the critical challenges and opportunities in developing and deploying intelligent systems capable of autonomous, robust, and ethical operation within complex clinical environments. While sophisticated AI agents promise to revolutionize patient care, diagnostics, research, and administration, significant hurdles in long-term memory and planning, grounded interaction, clinical reasoning, ethical alignment, multi-agent collaboration, tool use, robustness, personalization, multi-modality, and trustworthy evaluation persist.

AI in healthcare has advanced rapidly, with AI agents—systems capable of autonomous action, planning, and interaction—gaining prominence for complex, multi-step tasks and continual learning. Foundational works are outlining agent frameworks for healthcare. Yet, current agent capabilities often fall short of healthcare’s complex, high-stakes demands. The key challenges detailed below, spanning fundamental AI capabilities (memory, reasoning) to system-level concerns (multi-agent cooperation, tool use) and ethics/robustness/evaluation, represent critical research frontiers. This workshop will focus on addressing these critical research gaps:

  1. Memory & Continual Adaptation — maintain privacy-safe longitudinal memory, learn online from clinician feedback, and preserve a consistent persona across chronic care.
  2. Grounded, Multi-modal & Interactive Agents — ensure robust grounding across text, imaging, and sensor data while sustaining culturally appropriate, coherent dialogue over long encounters.
  3. Clinical Reasoning & Domain-specific Autonomy — achieve generalizable diagnostic reasoning under uncertainty, with privacy-preserving data access and clearly bounded autonomous actions that escalate safely to humans.
  4. Alignment, Values & Ethical Decision-making — align agents’ objectives with stakeholder values and moral trade-offs in real-time, open-ended tasks with resource constraints.
  5. Robustness, Global Generalization & Trustworthy Evaluation — develop agents and evaluation protocols that surface failures when systems are deployed beyond Western tertiary-care settings and majority-group datasets.
  6. Tool Use, Real-world Actuation & Multi-agent Collaboration — provide formal mechanisms for safe and well-coordinated multi-agent collaboration.
  7. Human–AI Collaboration and Workflow Integration — enable transparent hand-offs, explainable reasoning, and participatory development that fosters clinician and patient trust.
